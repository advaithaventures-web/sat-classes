Question Type 1

Words in Context

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Words in Context questions assess your ability to understand how words and phrases function in specific contexts. These Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions come in two formats:

  • Fill-in-the-blank format: You'll choose the word or phrase that best completes a sentence based on context clues.
  • Word meaning format: You'll identify what a specific underlined word means as used in the passage, not just its dictionary definition.

Success on these Digital SAT practice questions requires reading carefully for context clues, understanding connotation and tone, and recognizing how surrounding sentences influence word meaning. This is a fundamental skill for Digital SAT preparation.

Practice Question 1
Neurologist Oliver Sacks was known for his ability to ______ complex medical conditions in ways that general readers could understand, transforming technical case studies into compelling narratives that revealed the humanity of his patients.
Which choice completes the text with the most logical and precise word or phrase?
A. obscure
B. elucidate
C. complicate
D. summarize
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer because it most logically completes the text's discussion of Oliver Sacks's writing approach. "Elucidate" means to make something clear or explain it in detail. The text indicates that Sacks had an ability to help readers understand complex medical conditions by transforming technical information into compelling narratives. This context thus indicates that Sacks elucidated, or clarified, complex medical conditions for general readers.

Choice A is incorrect because "obscure" means to make something unclear or difficult to understand, which contradicts the text's description of Sacks making complex conditions understandable to general readers. Choice C is incorrect because "complicate" means to make something more complex or difficult, which is the opposite of what the text describes. Choice D is incorrect because while "summarize" might seem plausible, the text emphasizes that Sacks didn't just condense information but rather made it understandable through compelling narratives, which goes beyond mere summarization.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 2
The following text is from Zora Neale Hurston's 1937 novel Their Eyes Were Watching God.

The hurricane's force intensified as darkness fell. Families huddled in their hastily built shanties, constructed from salvaged wood and tin. The wind howled through gaps in the crude walls, which had been thrown together in haste and offered little protection against the storm's fury.
As used in the text, what does the word "crude" most nearly mean?
A. Unrefined
B. Offensive
C. Roughly made
D. Vulgar
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer because as used in the text, "crude" most nearly means "roughly made," or constructed in a basic, unpolished way. The text describes walls of shanties that were "thrown together in haste" from "salvaged wood and tin" and that had "gaps" through which wind howled. These details clearly indicate that the walls were roughly and quickly constructed, not carefully or skillfully built. Therefore, "crude walls" refers to walls that are roughly made.

Choice A is incorrect because while "unrefined" can be a synonym for crude in some contexts, it's too abstract for this context about physical construction. The text provides specific details about how the walls were made (hastily, from salvaged materials, with gaps), pointing to "roughly made" as the more precise meaning. Choice B is incorrect because "offensive" relates to crude language or behavior that is rude or inappropriate, not to the physical construction quality of walls. Choice D is incorrect because "vulgar" also relates to crude language or socially inappropriate behavior, not to how something is built.

Question Difficulty: Easy

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Words in Context

  • Always read the entire sentence and surrounding context before choosing your answer
  • Focus on how the word functions in this specific passage, not just its general dictionary meaning
  • Look for context clues in the same sentence and nearby sentences that hint at the word's meaning
  • For fill-in-the-blank questions, try each answer choice in the blank and see which creates the most logical meaning
  • Eliminate answers that contradict the passage's tone or message
  • Pay attention to transition words and phrases that signal relationships between ideas
Question Type 2

Text Structure and Purpose

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Text Structure and Purpose questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section evaluate your ability to understand how texts are organized and what they aim to accomplish. These Digital SAT practice questions appear in three formats:

  • Overall purpose: Identify what the entire text is trying to accomplish (explain, argue, describe, etc.)
  • Overall structure: Recognize how the text is organized (chronologically, compare/contrast, problem/solution, cause/effect)
  • Function of a portion: Understand what role a specific underlined section plays in supporting the text's main idea

These questions are essential for Digital SAT preparation because they test reading comprehension at a higher level—you must understand not just what the text says, but how and why it's constructed that way.

Practice Question 1
Marine biologist Sylvia Earle began her groundbreaking research on ocean ecosystems in the 1960s by conducting extensive underwater observations using early diving technology. She documented the behavior of marine species and the health of coral reefs in ways that had never been done before. Her work revealed alarming patterns of environmental degradation caused by pollution and overfishing. These findings prompted Earle to shift from pure research to environmental advocacy, leading her to establish Mission Blue, an organization dedicated to creating protected marine sanctuaries around the world.
Which choice best describes the overall structure of the text?
A. It presents a scientific theory, then describes experiments that tested that theory.
B. It traces the evolution of a scientist's career from research to activism, explaining what motivated the change.
C. It compares two different approaches to marine conservation, then advocates for one over the other.
D. It describes a problem in ocean ecosystems, then proposes multiple solutions to address it.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer. The text follows Sylvia Earle's career chronologically, beginning with her research work in the 1960s ("conducting extensive underwater observations"), then describing what her research revealed ("alarming patterns of environmental degradation"), and finally explaining how this led her to shift to activism ("prompted Earle to shift from pure research to environmental advocacy"). This traces the evolution from research to activism and explains the motivation for the change.

Choice A is incorrect. The text doesn't present a theory being tested; instead, it describes observational research and its consequences. Choice C is incorrect. The text doesn't compare two different approaches to marine conservation; it focuses solely on Earle's progression from researcher to advocate. Choice D is incorrect. While the text does mention problems in ocean ecosystems, its primary focus is on Earle's career trajectory, not on presenting multiple solutions to environmental problems.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 2
Historian Dolores Hayden argues that urban planning in twentieth-century America systematically excluded women's perspectives. City layouts prioritized men's needs for commuting to centralized workplaces while ignoring women's need for accessible community services, childcare facilities, and local employment opportunities. As evidence, Hayden points to the post-World War II suburban developments, where houses were built far from schools, shops, and medical facilities, creating environments that assumed women would be available to drive children and manage household errands throughout the day. This design philosophy reinforced traditional gender roles and limited women's economic independence.
Which choice best describes the function of the underlined portion in the text as a whole?
A. It provides a specific historical example that supports the historian's argument about gender bias in urban planning.
B. It introduces a counterargument to Hayden's main thesis about women and city design.
C. It explains the economic factors that influenced suburban development after World War II.
D. It describes the benefits that suburban design offered to families in the mid-twentieth century.
✓ Correct Answer: A

Rationale:

Choice A is the best answer because it best describes how the underlined portion functions in the text as a whole. The text begins by stating Hayden's argument that urban planning excluded women's perspectives and prioritized men's needs. The underlined portion provides a concrete example of this bias: post-World War II suburban developments that placed houses far from essential services, assuming women would be available for transportation duties. This specific example serves as evidence supporting Hayden's broader claim about gender bias in urban planning.

Choice B is incorrect because the underlined portion supports Hayden's thesis rather than presenting a counterargument to it; the suburban example reinforces her claim about gender bias. Choice C is incorrect because while the underlined portion mentions a time period (post-World War II), it doesn't explain economic factors that influenced development; instead, it focuses on how the design reflected gendered assumptions. Choice D is incorrect because the underlined portion emphasizes the problems with suburban design for women, not the benefits it offered to families.

Practice Question 3
In 2018, computer scientist Timnit Gebru and her colleague Joy Buolamwini published research demonstrating that facial recognition systems performed significantly worse at identifying women and people with darker skin tones. Their study tested commercial facial recognition software from major technology companies and found error rates of up to 34% for dark-skinned women, compared to less than 1% for light-skinned men. Since then, Gebru has continued to investigate algorithmic bias, examining how artificial intelligence systems can perpetuate discrimination. Her work has influenced policy discussions and led several cities to ban the use of facial recognition technology by law enforcement.
Which choice best describes the overall purpose of the text?
A. To argue that facial recognition technology should be completely banned worldwide.
B. To explain the technical mechanisms that cause errors in facial recognition software.
C. To present an overview of Gebru's research on algorithmic bias and its broader impact.
D. To compare the accuracy rates of different facial recognition systems across multiple companies.
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer. The text presents a summary of Timnit Gebru's research journey, starting with her 2018 study on facial recognition bias, describing her continued investigation of algorithmic bias, and concluding with the impact of her work on policy and law enforcement practices. This provides an overview of both her research and its broader societal influence.

Choice A is incorrect. This isn't the overall purpose. While the text mentions that some cities banned facial recognition technology, it presents this as one outcome of Gebru's research, not as an argument the text itself is making for a worldwide ban. Choice B is incorrect. The text doesn't explain the technical mechanisms behind the errors; instead, it focuses on documenting the existence of bias and its consequences. Choice D is incorrect. Although the text mentions that multiple companies' software was tested, comparing accuracy rates across companies is not the overall purpose; the text is focused on Gebru's research career and impact.

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Text Structure and Purpose

  • For structure questions, identify how ideas progress: chronologically, by comparison, cause/effect, or problem/solution
  • For purpose questions, ask yourself "What is this text trying to accomplish?" rather than "What is this text about?"
  • When analyzing underlined portions, consider how they connect to the text's main idea—do they provide evidence, examples, counterarguments, or elaboration?
  • Look for transition words and phrases that signal structure (however, therefore, for example, in contrast)
  • Eliminate answers that focus on minor details rather than the overall organization or purpose
  • Practice identifying whether a text primarily explains, argues, describes, or compares—this is crucial for Digital SAT Reading & Writing success
Question Type 3

Central Ideas & Details

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Central Ideas & Details questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section evaluate your ability to identify main ideas and locate key supporting details in passages. These Digital SAT practice questions come in several formats:

  • Main idea questions: "Which choice best states the main idea of the text?" - You must identify the passage's primary point, not just a supporting detail.
  • "Based on the text" questions: "Based on the text, what [challenge/idea/suggestion] is presented?" - These ask what the text indicates or suggests about a specific topic.
  • "According to the text" questions: "According to the text, what is one reason..." - These ask for specific information directly stated in the passage.

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires distinguishing between main ideas and supporting details, identifying what the text explicitly states versus what it implies, and avoiding answers that focus on minor points or go beyond what the text actually says. This is crucial for Digital SAT preparation.

Practice Question 1
The following text is adapted from Emily Dickinson's 1891 poem "Hope is the thing with feathers."

"Hope" is the thing with feathers -
That perches in the soul -
And sings the tune without the words -
And never stops - at all -

And sweetest - in the Gale - is heard -
And sore must be the storm -
That could abash the little Bird
That kept so many warm -

I've heard it in the chillest land -
And on the strangest Sea -
Yet - never - in Extremity,
It asked a crumb - of me.
Based on the text, what is suggested about hope?
A. It requires constant reinforcement from external sources to remain strong.
B. It persists even in the most difficult circumstances without demanding anything in return.
C. It becomes weakest when people face their greatest challenges.
D. It depends on positive experiences to sustain itself over time.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer. The text describes hope as something that "never stops," is heard even "in the Gale" (during storms), exists "in the chillest land" and "on the strangest Sea," and notably, "never - in Extremity, / It asked a crumb - of me." This suggests that hope persists through difficult circumstances ("Extremity," storms, harsh conditions) without requiring anything from the person experiencing it.

Choice A is incorrect. The text suggests the opposite: hope "never stops" on its own and doesn't ask for anything ("never...asked a crumb - of me"), indicating it doesn't need external reinforcement. Choice C is incorrect. The text states that hope is "sweetest - in the Gale" and continues even "in Extremity," suggesting it remains strong during challenges rather than becoming weak. Choice D is incorrect. The text emphasizes that hope persists through negative experiences (storms, "chillest land") without needing positive reinforcement, as it "never...asked a crumb."

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 2
In the 1950s, urban planner Jane Jacobs challenged prevailing theories about city design. While most experts advocated for wide streets, separated land uses, and the demolition of older neighborhoods to build modern high-rises, Jacobs argued that thriving cities needed the opposite: narrow streets that slowed traffic, mixed-use neighborhoods where people lived and worked, and preservation of older buildings that provided affordable spaces for small businesses. Her observations of Greenwich Village in New York City demonstrated that dense, diverse neighborhoods with short blocks created safer, more economically vibrant communities than the sterile modern developments other planners promoted. Jacobs's ideas, initially dismissed by establishment planners, eventually transformed urban planning philosophy and inspired movements to preserve historic neighborhoods in cities worldwide.
Which choice best states the main idea of the text?
A. Jane Jacobs proved that older buildings are always superior to modern architecture for creating successful urban environments.
B. Jane Jacobs's observations of Greenwich Village led city planners to completely abandon modern approaches to urban development.
C. Jane Jacobs challenged conventional urban planning theories by advocating for dense, mixed-use neighborhoods, and her ideas ultimately reshaped the field.
D. Jane Jacobs focused primarily on traffic management and demonstrated that narrow streets are the most important factor in successful city design.
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer because it most accurately states the main idea of the text. The text explains that Jacobs challenged prevailing theories (advocating for narrow streets, mixed-use neighborhoods, and older buildings instead of wide streets, separated uses, and modern high-rises), provides an example of her approach (Greenwich Village observations), and concludes that her ideas "transformed urban planning philosophy" and "inspired movements" worldwide. This captures both her challenge to conventional thinking and her lasting impact on the field.

Choice A is incorrect. This overstates Jacobs's position. While the text mentions she advocated for preserving older buildings for affordable spaces, it doesn't claim she proved older buildings are "always superior" to modern architecture. Choice B is incorrect. The text says her ideas were "initially dismissed" and "eventually transformed" the field, not that planners "completely abandoned" modern approaches. Choice D is incorrect. This focuses too narrowly on one detail. While narrow streets are mentioned, the text presents them as part of a broader philosophy about dense, diverse neighborhoods, not as "the most important factor."

Question Difficulty: Easy

Practice Question 3
Biologist Rachel Carson's 1962 book Silent Spring documented how widespread use of synthetic pesticides, particularly DDT, was harming bird populations and contaminating food chains. Chemical companies and many scientists initially attacked Carson's research, claiming she exaggerated the dangers and threatened agricultural productivity. Despite this opposition, Carson's meticulous documentation of pesticide impacts, drawn from hundreds of scientific studies, convinced the public and policymakers of the need for stronger environmental regulations. Her work directly led to the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency and the eventual ban on DDT in the United States, fundamentally changing how society approaches the relationship between chemical use and environmental health.
According to the text, what was one reason Carson's work was significant?
A. She discovered DDT and other synthetic pesticides were being used in agriculture.
B. She was the first scientist to study the effects of chemicals on bird populations.
C. Her research led to major policy changes including the creation of a federal environmental agency.
D. She convinced chemical companies to voluntarily stop producing harmful pesticides.
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer. The text explicitly states that Carson's work "directly led to the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency and the eventual ban on DDT in the United States," which are major policy changes. This clearly identifies a significant impact of her work.

Choice A is incorrect. The text doesn't say Carson discovered that pesticides were being used; rather, it says she "documented how widespread use" of them was causing harm. The text indicates the pesticides were already in widespread use when she studied them. Choice B is incorrect. The text doesn't claim Carson was the first to study chemical effects on birds. It says she documented these impacts and drew from "hundreds of scientific studies," suggesting other scientists had also conducted research in this area. Choice D is incorrect. The text states that "chemical companies and many scientists initially attacked Carson's research," and mentions government bans on DDT, not voluntary action by chemical companies to stop production.

Question Difficulty: Easy

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Central Ideas & Details

  • For main idea questions, ask yourself "What is this passage primarily about?" and eliminate answers that focus on minor details
  • The main idea should be supported by most or all of the passage, not just one sentence or example
  • Avoid answers that use extreme language like "always," "never," "completely," or "only" unless the text explicitly supports such claims
  • For "according to the text" questions, the answer must be directly stated—don't choose answers that require too much inference
  • For "based on the text" questions with poetry or literary passages, pay attention to metaphors and figurative language
  • Read all answer choices carefully—wrong answers often include accurate details that don't answer the specific question asked
  • Practice distinguishing between what the text explicitly states and what it suggests or implies
Question Type 4

Inferences

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Inferences questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section test your ability to draw logical conclusions from information presented in a passage. Unlike questions that ask what a text directly states, these Digital SAT practice questions require you to synthesize multiple pieces of evidence to reach a conclusion that logically follows but isn't explicitly stated.

These questions typically follow this format: a passage presents evidence, observations, or an experimental setup, ending with "______" and asking "Which choice most logically completes the text?"

Key characteristics of Digital SAT inference questions:

  • Evidence synthesis: You must combine multiple details from the passage to reach the logical conclusion
  • Cause-and-effect reasoning: Often involves understanding relationships between different pieces of information
  • Hypothesis completion: Some passages describe research expectations (e.g., "researchers suspected that...") and you must infer the logical outcome
  • No direct statements: The correct answer won't be explicitly stated—it must be inferred from the evidence provided

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires careful reading of all details, understanding logical relationships, and eliminating answers that go beyond what the passage supports. These are often among the most challenging questions in Digital SAT preparation.

Practice Question 1
Archaeologist Maria Gonzalez's excavation of a 15th-century settlement in Peru revealed an unexpected pattern: while the site contained abundant ceramic fragments typical of coastal communities, it also yielded substantial quantities of alpaca wool and tools for processing it—materials and implements associated exclusively with highland populations living over 200 kilometers away at significantly higher elevations. Notably, the wool showed no signs of having been processed into finished textiles at the coastal site; rather, it appeared to have arrived already woven. Additionally, chemical analysis of the ceramic fragments indicated that while most were produced locally using coastal clay, approximately 30% were manufactured using clay sources found only in highland regions. These findings suggest that ______
Which choice most logically completes the text?
A. coastal communities possessed the technology to replicate highland weaving techniques but chose not to use locally available materials.
B. the settlement served as a trading hub where coastal and highland communities exchanged specialized goods rather than raw materials.
C. highland populations had established permanent settlements in coastal areas and maintained their traditional craft practices.
D. coastal populations periodically migrated to highland regions to acquire materials unavailable in their home territories.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer because it presents the conclusion that most logically completes the text's discussion of the archaeological evidence from the Peruvian settlement. The text describes a pattern where the coastal site contained both coastal and highland materials, but crucially, the highland materials arrived in processed forms: the alpaca wool "appeared to have arrived already woven" rather than as raw wool that would be processed locally, and 30% of ceramics were "manufactured using clay sources found only in highland regions" rather than made locally from highland clay. This pattern indicates an exchange of finished, specialized products between communities. If the coastal site were simply acquiring raw materials, we would expect to find raw wool being processed there and local ceramics made from imported clay. Instead, the evidence points to communities trading their specialized finished goods—coastal communities producing coastal ceramics and highland communities producing woven textiles and highland ceramics—with the coastal settlement serving as a point where these specialized products were exchanged.

Choice A is incorrect because the text indicates that the wool "appeared to have arrived already woven," meaning coastal communities were receiving finished textiles rather than producing them locally. If coastal communities possessed highland weaving techniques and chose not to use local materials, we would expect to find raw alpaca wool at the site being processed into textiles, but the text explicitly states the wool arrived already woven. Choice C is incorrect because the evidence doesn't support permanent highland settlement in coastal areas. If highland populations had established permanent coastal settlements while maintaining their practices, we would expect to find evidence of wool processing at the coastal site (raw wool, processing tools, evidence of weaving), but the text indicates the wool arrived already woven, suggesting it was processed elsewhere and then transported. Additionally, the presence of both locally-produced coastal ceramics and highland-produced ceramics suggests two distinct communities trading rather than one community maintaining dual traditions. Choice D is incorrect because periodic coastal migration to highland regions would not explain the key pattern described: finished highland products (woven textiles, highland-made ceramics) appearing at the coastal site. Migration for material acquisition would more likely result in raw materials being transported back and processed locally, but the text emphasizes that highland goods arrived in finished form, indicating trade between settled communities rather than migration.

Question Difficulty: Hard

Practice Question 2
Psychologist David Chen observed that students who took brief breaks during long study sessions reported feeling more mentally refreshed and performed better on subsequent tests than students who studied continuously. Suspecting that the timing of these breaks was crucial to their effectiveness, Chen designed an experiment in which participants studied complex material for two hours. He anticipated that students who took breaks at regular 25-minute intervals would thus ______
Which choice most logically completes the text?
A. report feeling more fatigued than students who took breaks at irregular intervals.
B. demonstrate better retention of the material than students who took breaks only when they felt tired.
C. prefer longer study sessions without any breaks in future learning tasks.
D. perform similarly to students who took a single extended break halfway through the session.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer. It most logically completes the text. The text establishes that brief breaks during study sessions lead to better outcomes (feeling refreshed and better test performance). Chen suspected that "the timing of these breaks was crucial to their effectiveness," and designed an experiment with breaks at "regular 25-minute intervals." If timing is crucial, we can infer that Chen anticipated that scheduled, regular breaks would be more effective than breaks taken only when students felt tired (which would be irregular and potentially too late to prevent mental fatigue). Thus, students with regularly scheduled breaks would demonstrate better retention than those taking breaks based on feeling tired.

Choice A is incorrect. It doesn't logically complete the text. Chen's hypothesis was that timing matters for break effectiveness. If he anticipated that regular intervals would make students more fatigued than irregular intervals, this would contradict his observation that breaks improve outcomes and his belief that timing is crucial to effectiveness. Choice C is incorrect. It doesn't logically complete the text. The text establishes that breaks improve study outcomes, so Chen would not anticipate that effective breaks would lead students to prefer studying without breaks in the future. Choice D is incorrect. It doesn't logically complete the text. Chen's hypothesis was specifically that "timing of these breaks was crucial," which suggests he believed multiple regular breaks would be more effective than different break patterns. If he anticipated similar performance between regular intervals and a single extended break, this would contradict his premise that timing is crucial.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Inferences

  • Read the entire passage carefully before looking at answer choices—you need all the evidence to make the correct inference
  • Look for patterns, contrasts, or relationships between different pieces of information in the passage
  • The correct answer must be supported by evidence in the text, even though it's not directly stated
  • For hypothesis-testing passages, identify what the researcher suspects or expects, then choose the answer that logically follows
  • Eliminate answers that go beyond what the passage supports or contradict information given
  • Pay attention to qualifying words like "rather," "notably," "however"—these often signal important contrasts that lead to the inference
  • Practice distinguishing between what must be true based on the evidence versus what might be true but isn't necessarily supported
Question Type 5

Command of Evidence

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Command of Evidence questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section assess your ability to identify which piece of evidence best supports a given claim or hypothesis. These Digital SAT practice questions come in two formats:

  • Textual Evidence: Evaluate multiple potential findings and determine which one most directly supports a textual argument or hypothesis presented in the passage.
  • Quantitative Evidence: Interpret data from tables, charts, or graphs and identify which specific data points support or contradict a given hypothesis or claim.

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires understanding the logical relationship between claims and evidence, distinguishing between relevant and irrelevant support, and recognizing when evidence directly addresses the specific point being made. This is a critical skill for Digital SAT preparation and academic reasoning.

Part A: Textual Evidence

Practice Question 1
Neuroscientist Lucia Martinez has been studying the effects of bilingualism on cognitive flexibility—the brain's ability to switch between different tasks or mental frameworks. While previous research established that bilingual individuals often show advantages in executive function tasks, Martinez hypothesized that the cognitive benefits of bilingualism might extend specifically to creative problem-solving. She theorized that regularly switching between two language systems could enhance the brain's ability to approach problems from multiple perspectives, a key component of creative thinking. To test this hypothesis, Martinez designed a study comparing bilingual and monolingual adults on various creativity assessments.
Which finding from Martinez's study, if true, would most directly support her hypothesis?
A. Bilingual participants demonstrated faster reaction times than monolingual participants on standard executive function tests that measure task-switching speed.
B. Bilingual participants generated significantly more diverse solution strategies when presented with open-ended problems than monolingual participants did.
C. Both bilingual and monolingual participants showed similar levels of motivation and engagement when working on creative tasks.
D. Bilingual participants who learned their second language in early childhood performed better on memory tests than those who learned it later in life.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer because it presents a finding that, if true, would most directly support Martinez's hypothesis. The passage states that Martinez hypothesized bilingualism's cognitive benefits might extend specifically to creative problem-solving, and she theorized that switching between languages could enhance "the brain's ability to approach problems from multiple perspectives, a key component of creative thinking." Choice B directly addresses this hypothesis by showing that bilingual participants "generated significantly more diverse solution strategies" for open-ended problems. Generating diverse solution strategies is precisely what "approaching problems from multiple perspectives" means in practice, and this finding demonstrates enhanced creative problem-solving ability in bilingual individuals, which is exactly what Martinez's hypothesis predicted.

Choice A is incorrect because while it shows bilingual advantages in task-switching (an executive function), it doesn't address Martinez's specific hypothesis about creative problem-solving. The passage acknowledges that bilingual advantages in executive function were already established by previous research; Martinez's hypothesis was specifically about extending these findings to creativity. Faster reaction times on standard executive function tests don't demonstrate the creative problem-solving enhancement that Martinez predicted. Choice C is incorrect because similar levels of motivation and engagement between the groups doesn't support the hypothesis that bilingualism enhances creative problem-solving. This finding suggests the groups were comparable on a confounding variable (motivation), but it doesn't provide evidence that bilingual individuals show enhanced creativity. Choice D is incorrect because it addresses a comparison within the bilingual group (early vs. late learners) on memory tests, not a comparison between bilingual and monolingual groups on creative problem-solving. Martinez's hypothesis was about bilingualism's effect on creativity, not about when the second language was learned or about memory performance.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 2
When primatologist Jane Goodall began her research at Gombe Stream National Park in Tanzania in 1960, the scientific establishment maintained strict protocols that emphasized emotional detachment and prohibited attributing human-like qualities to animal subjects. Researchers were expected to refer to animals by numbers rather than names and to describe behaviors in purely mechanical terms. Goodall, however, rejected these conventions. She named the chimpanzees she studied, described their distinct personalities, and documented complex emotional behaviors like grief, joy, and familial bonds. Critics argued this approach was unscientific and overly anthropomorphic. According to science historian Rebecca Klein, Goodall's willingness to challenge these established protocols ultimately transformed primatology by demonstrating that recognizing animals' individual personalities and emotional lives could lead to deeper scientific insights rather than compromising objectivity.
Which finding from Goodall's research, if true, would most directly support Klein's claim?
A. Goodall's observations revealed that chimpanzees in the Gombe community lived longer on average than chimpanzees in other documented populations in East Africa.
B. Goodall's documentation of a chimpanzee named David Greybeard using modified twigs as tools to extract termites led to the redefinition of what distinguishes humans from other animals, as tool use was previously considered uniquely human.
C. Goodall's long-term presence at Gombe allowed her to observe the same individuals over decades, providing longitudinal data unavailable in shorter studies.
D. Goodall's approach of tracking individual chimpanzees she had named enabled her to identify that a female named Flo's nurturing parenting style directly influenced her offspring's social success, revealing how individual behavioral differences affect evolutionary outcomes.
✓ Correct Answer: D

Rationale:

Choice D is the best answer because it presents a finding that most directly supports Klein's claim that recognizing individual personalities could lead to deeper scientific insights. Klein's claim specifically addresses Goodall's controversial practice of naming animals and documenting their "distinct personalities" and "emotional behaviors," arguing this approach led to deeper insights rather than compromising science. Choice D describes a discovery that directly resulted from Goodall's individualized approach: by tracking and naming specific chimpanzees (Flo and her offspring), Goodall was able to connect an individual's personality trait (Flo's "nurturing parenting style") to significant scientific insights about social success and evolutionary outcomes. This finding demonstrates that recognizing individual personalities wasn't just acceptable but actually enabled scientific discoveries about how behavioral differences affect evolution—precisely the "deeper scientific insights" Klein claims resulted from Goodall's approach. The discovery wouldn't have been possible with the traditional numbered, depersonalized approach because researchers wouldn't have tracked individuals closely enough to notice personality-based parenting differences and their long-term effects.

Choice A is incorrect because while this represents a factual finding about the Gombe chimpanzees, it doesn't support Klein's specific claim about Goodall's methodology. The observation that Gombe chimpanzees lived longer doesn't demonstrate that recognizing individual personalities and emotional lives led to deeper insights; lifespan data could have been collected using traditional numbered identification methods without any attention to personality or emotion. This finding relates to the success of the Gombe site but not to the value of Goodall's controversial individualized approach. Choice B is incorrect because although this was indeed one of Goodall's most significant scientific contributions, it doesn't support Klein's claim about the value of recognizing individual personalities and emotions. The tool-use discovery—while revolutionary—could theoretically have been observed and documented using traditional methods with numbered subjects and mechanical behavioral descriptions. The finding doesn't demonstrate that naming animals or recognizing their personalities led to deeper insights; any careful observer using standard protocols could have documented tool use. Transitions

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Transitions questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section assess your ability to identify the logical relationship between sentences and choose the appropriate transitional word or phrase to connect ideas. These Digital SAT practice questions require you to understand how ideas flow within a passage and select transitions that accurately signal the relationship between consecutive sentences.

These questions follow a consistent format:

  • A passage with a blank between sentences: You'll see two sentences with "______" between them, indicating where a transition should go
  • The question asks: "Which choice completes the text with the most logical transition?"
  • Four transitional words or phrases: Each answer choice presents a different transition that signals a different logical relationship

Common transition types include:

  • Addition: "In addition," "Furthermore," "Moreover" - signal that the sentence adds to previous information
  • Contrast: "However," "By contrast," "Nevertheless" - signal that the sentence contrasts with or contradicts previous information
  • Clarification: "That is," "In other words," "Specifically" - signal that the sentence explains or clarifies previous information
  • Cause/Effect: "Therefore," "Thus," "Consequently" - signal that the sentence presents a result or consequence
  • Example: "For example," "For instance" - signal that the sentence provides an example of previous information

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires understanding the logical flow of ideas, not just memorizing transition definitions. This is a fundamental skill for Digital SAT preparation and academic writing.

Practice Question 1

B. In addition,
C. However,

Choice B is the best answer. "In addition," logically signals that the detail in the sentence after the blank—that Curie's work laid the foundation for modern nuclear physics and influenced multiple fields—adds to the information in the previous sentence. The transition "in addition" accurately signals this relationship of providing another significant contribution of Curie's work.

Choice A is incorrect because "for instance" illogically signals that the detail in this sentence is an example of the information in the previous sentence. Instead, the previous sentence discusses medical applications, while the sentence after the blank discusses entirely different applications in nuclear physics, energy, and materials science—this is additional information, not an example of medical treatment. Choice C is incorrect because "however" illogically signals that the detail in this sentence contrasts with the information in the previous sentence. Instead, both sentences present positive impacts of Curie's work; the sentence after the blank doesn't contradict the previous sentence but rather adds to it by describing impacts in different fields. Choice D is incorrect because "as a result" illogically signals that the detail in this sentence is a consequence of the information in the previous sentence. Instead, the sentence after the blank presents another simultaneous contribution of Curie's work, not a result of the medical applications mentioned previously. Both the medical impact and the influence on nuclear physics stemmed from Curie's discovery, rather than one causing the other.

Question Type 9

Form, Structure, and Sense

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Form, Structure, and Sense questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section assess your command of Standard English grammar and usage. These Digital SAT practice questions test whether you can recognize and correct errors in sentence structure, verb forms, and punctuation conventions.

These questions follow a consistent format:

  • A passage with a blank: You'll see a sentence or short passage with "______" indicating where an error needs correction
  • The question asks: "Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?"
  • Four answer choices: Each presents a different grammatical construction, but only one follows Standard English conventions

This question type covers four main grammatical concepts:

  • Subject-Modifier Placement: Modifiers must be placed next to the words they describe to avoid confusion. Misplaced modifiers create unclear or illogical sentences.
  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Verbs must agree in number with their subjects. Singular subjects take singular verbs; plural subjects take plural verbs. Pay special attention to gerunds (which are always singular) and be careful when phrases separate the subject from the verb.
  • Verb Tenses: Verb tenses must be consistent throughout a passage and appropriate for the time frame being described. When narrating past events, use past tense verbs consistently; when describing general truths or current situations, use present tense.
  • Apostrophes: Use apostrophes correctly to show possession (the student's book) but NOT with possessive pronouns (its, their, whose—never it's, they're, who's when showing possession). Also distinguish between plural nouns (students) and possessive nouns (student's or students').

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires understanding these core grammar rules and applying them carefully to identify the choice that produces a grammatically correct, clear sentence. This is essential for Digital SAT preparation and strong academic writing.

Practice Question 1: Subject-Modifier Placement
The International Space Station orbits Earth at an altitude of approximately 400 kilometers, traveling at a speed of 28,000 kilometers per hour. Designed to facilitate scientific research in microgravity, ______ has hosted astronauts from over twenty countries since its first module was launched in 1998.
Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?
A. the station
B. astronauts from multiple nations
C. scientific research in microgravity
D. over twenty countries have sent their astronauts there, and it
✓ Correct Answer: A

Rationale:

Choice A is the best answer because it correctly places the subject being modified immediately after the modifier. The opening modifier "Designed to facilitate scientific research in microgravity" describes the International Space Station, not the astronauts or the research itself. Modifiers must be placed next to the subjects they describe, so "the station" (referring back to the International Space Station) needs to be the first element after the comma. Choice A accomplishes this by making "the station" the subject that was "designed to facilitate scientific research," which is logically correct.

Choice B is incorrect because it creates a misplaced modifier error. The modifier "Designed to facilitate scientific research in microgravity" would be describing "astronauts from multiple nations," but astronauts were not designed to facilitate research—the station was. This creates an illogical sentence suggesting that the astronauts themselves were designed for this purpose, rather than the station being designed. Choice C is incorrect because it creates a misplaced modifier error and an unclear sentence. The phrase "scientific research in microgravity" cannot logically follow the modifier "Designed to facilitate scientific research" because research itself is not designed to facilitate research. Additionally, research cannot "host astronauts," making the sentence illogical. Choice D is incorrect because it creates a misplaced modifier error. Beginning with "over twenty countries" suggests that the countries were "designed to facilitate scientific research," which is illogical. The awkward construction with "and it" at the end further confuses the sentence structure.

Practice Question 2: Subject-Verb Agreement
Marine biologists studying whale communication have made remarkable discoveries about humpback whale songs. Recording and analyzing the complex vocalizations of humpback whales across different ocean regions ______ revealed that whale populations share and modify songs over time, suggesting a form of cultural transmission previously thought unique to humans.

A. have

Choice D is the best answer because it provides a singular verb that agrees with the singular subject. The subject of this sentence is "Recording and analyzing," which functions as a compound gerund phrase. When two gerunds are joined by "and" to describe a single, unified action or process, they are treated as singular and require a singular verb. In this case, "recording and analyzing" describes one integrated research process (not two separate activities), so it takes the singular verb "has." The phrase "Recording and analyzing...has revealed" is grammatically correct.

Although the gerund phrase contains the word "and," it describes a single unified research process, making it singular. Additionally, the phrase is followed by "the complex vocalizations" (plural object), which might tempt students to use a plural verb, but the object doesn't determine subject-verb agreement—only the subject does. Additionally, "are revealed" would create an awkward passive construction that doesn't fit the sentence's structure. Choice C is incorrect because "were" is a plural past tense verb that doesn't agree with the singular subject. Furthermore, the context of the sentence suggests ongoing research findings rather than completed past actions, making past tense inappropriate. The phrase "have made remarkable discoveries" at the beginning establishes present perfect tense, indicating research that continues to be relevant.

Practice Question 3: Verb Tenses
In 1969, computer scientist Margaret Hamilton led the team that developed the onboard flight software for NASA's Apollo 11 mission. Her innovative approach to software engineering, including her emphasis on rigorous testing and error prevention, ______ the spacecraft's guidance system to function flawlessly during the historic moon landing. Hamilton's contributions fundamentally shaped how software engineering is practiced today.
Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?
A. has enabled
B. will enable
C. enables
D. enabled
✓ Correct Answer: D

Rationale:

Choice D is the best answer because the past tense verb "enabled" is consistent with the other past tense verbs used to narrate these historical events from 1969. The passage describes events that occurred during the Apollo 11 mission using past tense: "led" (past tense) and later "shaped" (past tense). The sentence is narrating how Hamilton's approach helped the spacecraft's guidance system function during the 1969 moon landing—a specific completed action in the past. "Enabled" correctly maintains this consistent past tense throughout the passage, creating a coherent narrative about historical events.

Choice A is incorrect because "has enabled" is present perfect tense, which emphasizes a connection between past actions and present results. While present perfect could work for describing Hamilton's lasting influence (as in the final sentence about how she "shaped" modern practices), it's inappropriate here because the sentence specifically describes what happened "during the historic moon landing"—a completed past event. The present perfect tense would incorrectly suggest an ongoing enabling action. Choice B is incorrect because "will enable" is future tense, which is completely inconsistent with the past tense narrative about events that occurred in 1969. Future tense indicates something that hasn't happened yet, but the passage is narrating specific historical events that already occurred during the moon landing. This would create an illogical time shift. Choice C is incorrect because "enables" is simple present tense, which is inconsistent with the past tense narrative. Simple present tense is used for habitual actions, general truths, or current states, not for narrating specific historical events that occurred in 1969. Using "enables" would create an awkward and illogical shift from past to present tense.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 4: Apostrophes
The ancient library of Alexandria was one of the largest and most significant libraries of the classical world. Scholars traveled from across the Mediterranean to study ______ vast collection of manuscripts, which reportedly contained works by Aristotle, Euclid, and Homer. The library's destruction, whether by fire or gradual decline, remains one of history's greatest losses of knowledge.
Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?
A. its
B. it's
C. their
D. there
✓ Correct Answer: A

Rationale:

Choice A is the best answer because the singular possessive pronoun "its" agrees with the singular antecedent "The ancient library" and correctly indicates possession without using an apostrophe. Possessive pronouns (its, their, whose, your) never use apostrophes. In this sentence, "its vast collection" shows that the collection belongs to the library. The pronoun must be singular ("its" not "their") because the antecedent is singular: "The ancient library of Alexandria." The sentence is discussing one library's collection, not multiple libraries' collections.

Choice B is incorrect because "it's" is a contraction of "it is," which would create an ungrammatical sentence. The sentence would read "Scholars traveled...to study it is vast collection," which doesn't make sense. The sentence needs a possessive pronoun to show that the collection belongs to the library, not a contraction. Remember: possessive pronouns never use apostrophes, while contractions always do. Choice C is incorrect because "their" is a plural possessive pronoun, but the antecedent "The ancient library" is singular. This creates a pronoun-antecedent agreement error. The sentence is about one library (the library of Alexandria), not multiple libraries, so a singular pronoun is required. Students might be tempted by this because "scholars" (plural) appears nearby, but the collection belongs to the library, not to the scholars. Choice D is incorrect because "there" is an adverb indicating location or an expletive beginning sentences (as in "there are"), not a possessive pronoun. "Their" (with an 'i') is the possessive pronoun, but even that would be wrong here because it's plural. "There" doesn't show possession at all and would make the sentence read "to study there vast collection," which is ungrammatical.

Question Difficulty: Easy

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Form, Structure, and Sense

  • Subject-Modifier Placement: Always place modifying phrases next to the word they describe—check what word comes immediately after the comma
  • Subject-Modifier Placement: If an opening modifier describes an action or characteristic, the subject performing that action must come right after the comma
  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Identify the true subject of the sentence—ignore intervening phrases that might confuse you
  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Remember that gerunds (verb forms ending in -ing used as nouns) are always singular, even when followed by plural objects
  • Subject-Verb Agreement: Singular subjects require singular verbs (is, was, has); plural subjects require plural verbs (are, were, have)
  • Verb Tenses: Maintain consistent verb tense throughout a passage—if narrating past events, use past tense consistently
  • Verb Tenses: Pay attention to time markers (dates, phrases like "in 1969" or "currently") that signal which tense is appropriate
  • Apostrophes: Possessive pronouns (its, their, whose, your) NEVER use apostrophes—contractions (it's = it is, they're = they are) do use apostrophes
  • Apostrophes: Check pronoun-antecedent agreement—singular antecedents need singular pronouns (its), plural antecedents need plural pronouns (their)
  • General Strategy: Read the entire sentence with each answer choice to see which creates the most grammatically correct and logical sentence
Question Type 10

Boundaries

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Boundaries questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section assess your ability to use commas, semicolons, colons, and dashes correctly to establish clear relationships between clauses, phrases, and sentence elements. These Digital SAT practice questions test whether you can identify appropriate punctuation to clarify meaning and follow Standard English conventions.

The Four Punctuation Marks and Their Uses:

1. COMMA USAGE:

  • Use commas when: (a) combining a dependent clause with an independent clause, (b) joining two independent clauses with FANBOYS conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so), (c) after introductory phrases, (d) after opening modifiers, (e) separating items in a list, (f) surrounding non-restrictive (supplementary) information
  • Do NOT use commas: between subject and verb, between only TWO independent clauses without a conjunction (comma splice error), with restrictive (essential) information, between only TWO coordinates joined by "and," when title/description comes before a name, or mixed with dashes for the same element

2. SEMICOLON USAGE:

  • Use semicolons to: (a) join two closely related independent clauses without a conjunction, (b) serve as "super commas" separating items in complex lists when items contain internal commas
  • Do NOT use semicolons: with FANBOYS conjunctions (use comma + conjunction), between a main clause and a dependent/participial phrase, or to introduce lists or explanations (use colons instead)

3. COLON USAGE:

  • Use colons to: (a) join two independent clauses when the second explains/elaborates on the first, (b) introduce a list (the clause before the colon must be independent)
  • Do NOT use colons: to introduce relative clauses, between a verb and its object, or to separate elements that don't have an explanatory relationship

4. DASH USAGE:

  • Use dashes: as a pair to enclose/surround parenthetical or interrupting information (commonly tested); single dashes to introduce emphatic information are rarely tested
  • Do NOT mix punctuation: must use matching marks (comma-comma OR dash-dash, NOT comma-dash)

Critical Testing Patterns:

  • Comma Splice Error (Most Common): Students incorrectly use only a comma to join two independent clauses without a conjunction
  • Dependent vs. Independent Clause Recognition: Students confuse participial phrases (like "producing more than 350 paintings") with complete independent clauses. Participial phrases and gerunds without helping verbs are NOT independent clauses
  • Restrictive vs. Non-Restrictive Information: Essential information identifying WHICH one (restrictive) needs NO commas; supplementary information (non-restrictive) NEEDS commas
  • Matching Punctuation: Parenthetical information must be enclosed with matching punctuation—both commas or both dashes, never mixed

What College Board Does NOT Test:

  • Choosing between semicolon, colon, OR comma+FANBOYS to join independent clauses (all three are equally valid)

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires recognizing comma splices, understanding clause types (independent vs. dependent), distinguishing essential from supplementary information, and identifying when matching punctuation is needed. This is essential for Digital SAT preparation and clear academic writing.

Practice Question 1: Comma Usage & Clause Recognition
Archaeologist Kathleen Kenyon revolutionized excavation methods in the 1950s by introducing stratigraphic analysis to Middle Eastern sites. Her meticulous technique involved dividing excavation areas into precise grid ______ recording the exact position and depth of every artifact discovered, this approach allowed researchers to understand the chronological sequence of ancient civilizations with unprecedented accuracy.
Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?
A. squares,
B. squares and
C. squares, and
D. squares
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer because it correctly uses a comma with the coordinating conjunction "and" to join two independent clauses. The first clause "Her meticulous technique involved dividing excavation areas into precise grid squares" is a complete sentence with subject ("technique") and verb ("involved"). The second clause "recording the exact position and depth of every artifact discovered, this approach allowed researchers to understand the chronological sequence" begins with what appears to be a participial phrase ("recording...") but continues to form a complete independent clause with its own subject ("this approach") and verb ("allowed"). Since these are two independent clauses describing coordinate actions in Kenyon's technique, they require a comma plus a coordinating conjunction to join them properly.

Choice A is incorrect because it creates a comma splice—using only a comma to join two independent clauses without a coordinating conjunction. While "recording" might initially appear to be a participial phrase, the sentence continues with "this approach allowed," creating an independent clause with its own subject and verb. A comma alone cannot join two independent clauses; this is one of the most common punctuation errors tested on the Digital SAT. Choice B is incorrect because it fails to use a comma before the coordinating conjunction "and" when joining two independent clauses. Standard English requires a comma before FANBOYS conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) when they connect independent clauses. Without the comma, the sentence incorrectly runs the two clauses together. Choice D is incorrect because it provides no punctuation at all between two independent clauses, creating a run-on sentence. The sentence runs together "involved dividing excavation areas into precise grid squares recording the exact position and depth," which is grammatically incorrect and confusing. Some form of punctuation is required to establish the boundary between the two independent clauses and show their coordinate relationship.

Question Difficulty: Medium-Hard

Practice Question 2: Semicolon Usage in Complex Lists
Historian Pura Belpré made significant contributions to children's literature and library services in twentieth-century New York. She wrote bilingual folktales that preserved Puerto Rican ______ established the first Spanish-language story hour program at the New York Public Library, which served immigrant families; and mentored young Latina librarians, helping them navigate professional advancement in an era of limited opportunities.
Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?
A. culture,
B. culture;
C. culture, and
D. culture and
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer because it correctly uses semicolons to separate items in a complex list where the items themselves contain internal commas. The sentence describes three of Belpré's contributions: (1) "She wrote bilingual folktales that preserved Puerto Rican culture," (2) "established the first Spanish-language story hour program at the New York Public Library, which served immigrant families," and (3) "mentored young Latina librarians, helping them navigate professional advancement in an era of limited opportunities." The second and third items each contain internal commas that provide essential information ("New York Public Library, which served immigrant families" and "young Latina librarians, helping them navigate"). When list items contain internal commas, semicolons must be used to separate the main items to avoid confusion about where one accomplishment ends and the next begins. Using semicolons as "super commas" makes the structure clear: three major contributions separated by semicolons, with internal details separated by regular commas.

Choice A is incorrect because using only commas throughout creates ambiguity in a complex list. With commas alone, it becomes unclear whether "which served immigrant families" is part of the second contribution or describes something else, and whether "helping them navigate professional advancement" is part of the third contribution or a separate element. When list items contain internal commas, regular commas cannot effectively separate the main items—semicolons are required to establish clear boundaries between the primary elements. Choice C is incorrect because it uses a comma followed by "and" before the second item, which disrupts the parallel structure of the three-part list. In a list with three items containing internal commas, the pattern should be: "Item 1; Item 2; and Item 3," not "Item 1, and Item 2; and Item 3." This choice also fails to use the necessary semicolon to separate the first two complex items. Choice D is incorrect because it provides no punctuation between the first and second items, creating a run-on construction. Without any punctuation separating "culture" and "established," the sentence incorrectly runs together two distinct contributions: writing folktales and establishing a program. Even in a simple list, some form of punctuation would be necessary to separate items. The complete absence of punctuation makes it impossible to distinguish where the first contribution ends and the second begins.

Question Difficulty: Hard

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Boundaries

  • Comma Usage: Use commas with FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) to join two independent clauses
  • Comma Usage: Use pairs of commas to enclose non-restrictive (supplementary) information
  • Comma Usage: NEVER use only a comma to join two independent clauses without a conjunction—this creates a comma splice error
  • Semicolon Usage: Use semicolons to join two closely related independent clauses—both sides must be complete sentences
  • Semicolon Usage: Use semicolons as "super commas" in complex lists where items contain internal commas
  • Colon Usage: Use colons when the second clause explains/elaborates on the first, or to introduce lists
  • Dash Usage: Use matching pairs of dashes to enclose interrupting parenthetical information
  • Critical Strategy: Identify whether clauses are independent (can stand alone) or dependent
  • Critical Strategy: Watch for participial phrases that LOOK like verbs but aren't complete independent clauses
  • Critical Strategy: Determine if information is restrictive (essential—NO commas) or non-restrictive (supplementary—NEEDS commas)
  • Remember: College Board does NOT test choosing between semicolon vs. colon vs. comma+FANBOYS for joining independent clauses—all are equally valid
