Question Type 1

Words in Context

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Words in Context questions assess your ability to understand how words and phrases function in specific contexts. These Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions come in two formats:

  • Fill-in-the-blank format: You'll choose the word or phrase that best completes a sentence based on context clues.
  • Word meaning format: You'll identify what a specific underlined word means as used in the passage, not just its dictionary definition.

Success on these Digital SAT practice questions requires reading carefully for context clues, understanding connotation and tone, and recognizing how surrounding sentences influence word meaning. This is a fundamental skill for Digital SAT preparation.

Practice Question 1
Neurologist Oliver Sacks was known for his ability to ______ complex medical conditions in ways that general readers could understand, transforming technical case studies into compelling narratives that revealed the humanity of his patients.
Which choice completes the text with the most logical and precise word or phrase?
A. obscure
B. elucidate
C. complicate
D. summarize
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer because it most logically completes the text's discussion of Oliver Sacks's writing approach. "Elucidate" means to make something clear or explain it in detail. The text indicates that Sacks had an ability to help readers understand complex medical conditions by transforming technical information into compelling narratives. This context thus indicates that Sacks elucidated, or clarified, complex medical conditions for general readers.

Choice A is incorrect because "obscure" means to make something unclear or difficult to understand, which contradicts the text's description of Sacks making complex conditions understandable to general readers. Choice C is incorrect because "complicate" means to make something more complex or difficult, which is the opposite of what the text describes. Choice D is incorrect because while "summarize" might seem plausible, the text emphasizes that Sacks didn't just condense information but rather made it understandable through compelling narratives, which goes beyond mere summarization.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 2
The following text is from Zora Neale Hurston's 1937 novel Their Eyes Were Watching God.

The hurricane's force intensified as darkness fell. Families huddled in their hastily built shanties, constructed from salvaged wood and tin. The wind howled through gaps in the crude walls, which had been thrown together in haste and offered little protection against the storm's fury.
As used in the text, what does the word "crude" most nearly mean?
A. Unrefined
B. Offensive
C. Roughly made
D. Vulgar
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer because as used in the text, "crude" most nearly means "roughly made," or constructed in a basic, unpolished way. The text describes walls of shanties that were "thrown together in haste" from "salvaged wood and tin" and that had "gaps" through which wind howled. These details clearly indicate that the walls were roughly and quickly constructed, not carefully or skillfully built. Therefore, "crude walls" refers to walls that are roughly made.

Choice A is incorrect because while "unrefined" can be a synonym for crude in some contexts, it's too abstract for this context about physical construction. The text provides specific details about how the walls were made (hastily, from salvaged materials, with gaps), pointing to "roughly made" as the more precise meaning. Choice B is incorrect because "offensive" relates to crude language or behavior that is rude or inappropriate, not to the physical construction quality of walls. Choice D is incorrect because "vulgar" also relates to crude language or socially inappropriate behavior, not to how something is built.

Question Difficulty: Easy

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Words in Context

  • Always read the entire sentence and surrounding context before choosing your answer
  • Focus on how the word functions in this specific passage, not just its general dictionary meaning
  • Look for context clues in the same sentence and nearby sentences that hint at the word's meaning
  • For fill-in-the-blank questions, try each answer choice in the blank and see which creates the most logical meaning
  • Eliminate answers that contradict the passage's tone or message
  • Pay attention to transition words and phrases that signal relationships between ideas
Question Type 2

Text Structure and Purpose

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Text Structure and Purpose questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section evaluate your ability to understand how texts are organized and what they aim to accomplish. These Digital SAT practice questions appear in three formats:

  • Overall purpose: Identify what the entire text is trying to accomplish (explain, argue, describe, etc.)
  • Overall structure: Recognize how the text is organized (chronologically, compare/contrast, problem/solution, cause/effect)
  • Function of a portion: Understand what role a specific underlined section plays in supporting the text's main idea

These questions are essential for Digital SAT preparation because they test reading comprehension at a higher level—you must understand not just what the text says, but how and why it's constructed that way.

Practice Question 1
Marine biologist Sylvia Earle began her groundbreaking research on ocean ecosystems in the 1960s by conducting extensive underwater observations using early diving technology. She documented the behavior of marine species and the health of coral reefs in ways that had never been done before. Her work revealed alarming patterns of environmental degradation caused by pollution and overfishing. These findings prompted Earle to shift from pure research to environmental advocacy, leading her to establish Mission Blue, an organization dedicated to creating protected marine sanctuaries around the world.
Which choice best describes the overall structure of the text?
A. It presents a scientific theory, then describes experiments that tested that theory.
B. It traces the evolution of a scientist's career from research to activism, explaining what motivated the change.
C. It compares two different approaches to marine conservation, then advocates for one over the other.
D. It describes a problem in ocean ecosystems, then proposes multiple solutions to address it.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer. The text follows Sylvia Earle's career chronologically, beginning with her research work in the 1960s ("conducting extensive underwater observations"), then describing what her research revealed ("alarming patterns of environmental degradation"), and finally explaining how this led her to shift to activism ("prompted Earle to shift from pure research to environmental advocacy"). This traces the evolution from research to activism and explains the motivation for the change.

Choice A is incorrect. The text doesn't present a theory being tested; instead, it describes observational research and its consequences. Choice C is incorrect. The text doesn't compare two different approaches to marine conservation; it focuses solely on Earle's progression from researcher to advocate. Choice D is incorrect. While the text does mention problems in ocean ecosystems, its primary focus is on Earle's career trajectory, not on presenting multiple solutions to environmental problems.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 2
Historian Dolores Hayden argues that urban planning in twentieth-century America systematically excluded women's perspectives. City layouts prioritized men's needs for commuting to centralized workplaces while ignoring women's need for accessible community services, childcare facilities, and local employment opportunities. As evidence, Hayden points to the post-World War II suburban developments, where houses were built far from schools, shops, and medical facilities, creating environments that assumed women would be available to drive children and manage household errands throughout the day. This design philosophy reinforced traditional gender roles and limited women's economic independence.
Which choice best describes the function of the underlined portion in the text as a whole?
A. It provides a specific historical example that supports the historian's argument about gender bias in urban planning.
B. It introduces a counterargument to Hayden's main thesis about women and city design.
C. It explains the economic factors that influenced suburban development after World War II.
D. It describes the benefits that suburban design offered to families in the mid-twentieth century.
✓ Correct Answer: A

Rationale:

Choice A is the best answer because it best describes how the underlined portion functions in the text as a whole. The text begins by stating Hayden's argument that urban planning excluded women's perspectives and prioritized men's needs. The underlined portion provides a concrete example of this bias: post-World War II suburban developments that placed houses far from essential services, assuming women would be available for transportation duties. This specific example serves as evidence supporting Hayden's broader claim about gender bias in urban planning.

Choice B is incorrect because the underlined portion supports Hayden's thesis rather than presenting a counterargument to it; the suburban example reinforces her claim about gender bias. Choice C is incorrect because while the underlined portion mentions a time period (post-World War II), it doesn't explain economic factors that influenced development; instead, it focuses on how the design reflected gendered assumptions. Choice D is incorrect because the underlined portion emphasizes the problems with suburban design for women, not the benefits it offered to families.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 3
In 2018, computer scientist Timnit Gebru and her colleague Joy Buolamwini published research demonstrating that facial recognition systems performed significantly worse at identifying women and people with darker skin tones. Their study tested commercial facial recognition software from major technology companies and found error rates of up to 34% for dark-skinned women, compared to less than 1% for light-skinned men. Since then, Gebru has continued to investigate algorithmic bias, examining how artificial intelligence systems can perpetuate discrimination. Her work has influenced policy discussions and led several cities to ban the use of facial recognition technology by law enforcement.
Which choice best describes the overall purpose of the text?
A. To argue that facial recognition technology should be completely banned worldwide.
B. To explain the technical mechanisms that cause errors in facial recognition software.
C. To present an overview of Gebru's research on algorithmic bias and its broader impact.
D. To compare the accuracy rates of different facial recognition systems across multiple companies.
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer. The text presents a summary of Timnit Gebru's research journey, starting with her 2018 study on facial recognition bias, describing her continued investigation of algorithmic bias, and concluding with the impact of her work on policy and law enforcement practices. This provides an overview of both her research and its broader societal influence.

Choice A is incorrect. This isn't the overall purpose. While the text mentions that some cities banned facial recognition technology, it presents this as one outcome of Gebru's research, not as an argument the text itself is making for a worldwide ban. Choice B is incorrect. The text doesn't explain the technical mechanisms behind the errors; instead, it focuses on documenting the existence of bias and its consequences. Choice D is incorrect. This is too narrow. Although the text mentions that multiple companies' software was tested, comparing accuracy rates across companies is not the overall purpose; the text is focused on Gebru's research career and impact.

Question Difficulty: Easy

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Text Structure and Purpose

  • For structure questions, identify how ideas progress: chronologically, by comparison, cause/effect, or problem/solution
  • For purpose questions, ask yourself "What is this text trying to accomplish?" rather than "What is this text about?"
  • When analyzing underlined portions, consider how they connect to the text's main idea—do they provide evidence, examples, counterarguments, or elaboration?
  • Look for transition words and phrases that signal structure (however, therefore, for example, in contrast)
  • Eliminate answers that focus on minor details rather than the overall organization or purpose
  • Practice identifying whether a text primarily explains, argues, describes, or compares—this is crucial for Digital SAT Reading & Writing success
Question Type 3

Central Ideas & Details

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Central Ideas & Details questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section evaluate your ability to identify main ideas and locate key supporting details in passages. These Digital SAT practice questions come in several formats:

  • Main idea questions: "Which choice best states the main idea of the text?" - You must identify the passage's primary point, not just a supporting detail.
  • "Based on the text" questions: "Based on the text, what [challenge/idea/suggestion] is presented?" - These ask what the text indicates or suggests about a specific topic.
  • "According to the text" questions: "According to the text, what is one reason..." - These ask for specific information directly stated in the passage.

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires distinguishing between main ideas and supporting details, identifying what the text explicitly states versus what it implies, and avoiding answers that focus on minor points or go beyond what the text actually says. This is crucial for Digital SAT preparation.

Practice Question 1
The following text is adapted from Emily Dickinson's 1891 poem "Hope is the thing with feathers."

"Hope" is the thing with feathers -
That perches in the soul -
And sings the tune without the words -
And never stops - at all -

And sweetest - in the Gale - is heard -
And sore must be the storm -
That could abash the little Bird
That kept so many warm -

I've heard it in the chillest land -
And on the strangest Sea -
Yet - never - in Extremity,
It asked a crumb - of me.
Based on the text, what is suggested about hope?
A. It requires constant reinforcement from external sources to remain strong.
B. It persists even in the most difficult circumstances without demanding anything in return.
C. It becomes weakest when people face their greatest challenges.
D. It depends on positive experiences to sustain itself over time.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer. The text describes hope as something that "never stops," is heard even "in the Gale" (during storms), exists "in the chillest land" and "on the strangest Sea," and notably, "never - in Extremity, / It asked a crumb - of me." This suggests that hope persists through difficult circumstances ("Extremity," storms, harsh conditions) without requiring anything from the person experiencing it.

Choice A is incorrect. The text suggests the opposite: hope "never stops" on its own and doesn't ask for anything ("never...asked a crumb - of me"), indicating it doesn't need external reinforcement. Choice C is incorrect. The text states that hope is "sweetest - in the Gale" and continues even "in Extremity," suggesting it remains strong during challenges rather than becoming weak. Choice D is incorrect. The text emphasizes that hope persists through negative experiences (storms, "chillest land") without needing positive reinforcement, as it "never...asked a crumb."

Question Difficulty: Medium

Practice Question 2
In the 1950s, urban planner Jane Jacobs challenged prevailing theories about city design. While most experts advocated for wide streets, separated land uses, and the demolition of older neighborhoods to build modern high-rises, Jacobs argued that thriving cities needed the opposite: narrow streets that slowed traffic, mixed-use neighborhoods where people lived and worked, and preservation of older buildings that provided affordable spaces for small businesses. Her observations of Greenwich Village in New York City demonstrated that dense, diverse neighborhoods with short blocks created safer, more economically vibrant communities than the sterile modern developments other planners promoted. Jacobs's ideas, initially dismissed by establishment planners, eventually transformed urban planning philosophy and inspired movements to preserve historic neighborhoods in cities worldwide.
Which choice best states the main idea of the text?
A. Jane Jacobs proved that older buildings are always superior to modern architecture for creating successful urban environments.
B. Jane Jacobs's observations of Greenwich Village led city planners to completely abandon modern approaches to urban development.
C. Jane Jacobs challenged conventional urban planning theories by advocating for dense, mixed-use neighborhoods, and her ideas ultimately reshaped the field.
D. Jane Jacobs focused primarily on traffic management and demonstrated that narrow streets are the most important factor in successful city design.
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer because it most accurately states the main idea of the text. The text explains that Jacobs challenged prevailing theories (advocating for narrow streets, mixed-use neighborhoods, and older buildings instead of wide streets, separated uses, and modern high-rises), provides an example of her approach (Greenwich Village observations), and concludes that her ideas "transformed urban planning philosophy" and "inspired movements" worldwide. This captures both her challenge to conventional thinking and her lasting impact on the field.

Choice A is incorrect. This overstates Jacobs's position. While the text mentions she advocated for preserving older buildings for affordable spaces, it doesn't claim she proved older buildings are "always superior" to modern architecture. Choice B is incorrect. The text says her ideas were "initially dismissed" and "eventually transformed" the field, not that planners "completely abandoned" modern approaches. Choice D is incorrect. This focuses too narrowly on one detail. While narrow streets are mentioned, the text presents them as part of a broader philosophy about dense, diverse neighborhoods, not as "the most important factor."

Question Difficulty: Easy

Practice Question 3
Biologist Rachel Carson's 1962 book Silent Spring documented how widespread use of synthetic pesticides, particularly DDT, was harming bird populations and contaminating food chains. Chemical companies and many scientists initially attacked Carson's research, claiming she exaggerated the dangers and threatened agricultural productivity. Despite this opposition, Carson's meticulous documentation of pesticide impacts, drawn from hundreds of scientific studies, convinced the public and policymakers of the need for stronger environmental regulations. Her work directly led to the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency and the eventual ban on DDT in the United States, fundamentally changing how society approaches the relationship between chemical use and environmental health.
According to the text, what was one reason Carson's work was significant?
A. She discovered DDT and other synthetic pesticides were being used in agriculture.
B. She was the first scientist to study the effects of chemicals on bird populations.
C. Her research led to major policy changes including the creation of a federal environmental agency.
D. She convinced chemical companies to voluntarily stop producing harmful pesticides.
✓ Correct Answer: C

Rationale:

Choice C is the best answer. The text explicitly states that Carson's work "directly led to the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency and the eventual ban on DDT in the United States," which are major policy changes. This clearly identifies a significant impact of her work.

Choice A is incorrect. The text doesn't say Carson discovered that pesticides were being used; rather, it says she "documented how widespread use" of them was causing harm. The text indicates the pesticides were already in widespread use when she studied them. Choice B is incorrect. The text doesn't claim Carson was the first to study chemical effects on birds. It says she documented these impacts and drew from "hundreds of scientific studies," suggesting other scientists had also conducted research in this area. Choice D is incorrect. The text states that "chemical companies and many scientists initially attacked Carson's research," and mentions government bans on DDT, not voluntary action by chemical companies to stop production.

Question Difficulty: Easy

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Central Ideas & Details

  • For main idea questions, ask yourself "What is this passage primarily about?" and eliminate answers that focus on minor details
  • The main idea should be supported by most or all of the passage, not just one sentence or example
  • Avoid answers that use extreme language like "always," "never," "completely," or "only" unless the text explicitly supports such claims
  • For "according to the text" questions, the answer must be directly stated—don't choose answers that require too much inference
  • For "based on the text" questions with poetry or literary passages, pay attention to metaphors and figurative language
  • Read all answer choices carefully—wrong answers often include accurate details that don't answer the specific question asked
  • Practice distinguishing between what the text explicitly states and what it suggests or implies
Question Type 4

Inferences

What This Digital SAT Question Type Tests

Inferences questions on the Digital SAT Reading & Writing section test your ability to draw logical conclusions from information presented in a passage. Unlike questions that ask what a text directly states, these Digital SAT practice questions require you to synthesize multiple pieces of evidence to reach a conclusion that logically follows but isn't explicitly stated.

These questions typically follow this format: a passage presents evidence, observations, or an experimental setup, ending with "______" and asking "Which choice most logically completes the text?"

Key characteristics of Digital SAT inference questions:

  • Evidence synthesis: You must combine multiple details from the passage to reach the logical conclusion
  • Cause-and-effect reasoning: Often involves understanding relationships between different pieces of information
  • Hypothesis completion: Some passages describe research expectations (e.g., "researchers suspected that...") and you must infer the logical outcome
  • No direct statements: The correct answer won't be explicitly stated—it must be inferred from the evidence provided

Success on these Digital SAT Reading & Writing questions requires careful reading of all details, understanding logical relationships, and eliminating answers that go beyond what the passage supports. These are often among the most challenging questions in Digital SAT preparation.

Practice Question 1
Archaeologist Maria Gonzalez's excavation of a 15th-century settlement in Peru revealed an unexpected pattern: while the site contained abundant ceramic fragments typical of coastal communities, it also yielded substantial quantities of alpaca wool and tools for processing it—materials and implements associated exclusively with highland populations living over 200 kilometers away at significantly higher elevations. Notably, the wool showed no signs of having been processed into finished textiles at the coastal site; rather, it appeared to have arrived already woven. Additionally, chemical analysis of the ceramic fragments indicated that while most were produced locally using coastal clay, approximately 30% were manufactured using clay sources found only in highland regions. These findings suggest that ______
Which choice most logically completes the text?
A. coastal communities possessed the technology to replicate highland weaving techniques but chose not to use locally available materials.
B. the settlement served as a trading hub where coastal and highland communities exchanged specialized goods rather than raw materials.
C. highland populations had established permanent settlements in coastal areas and maintained their traditional craft practices.
D. coastal populations periodically migrated to highland regions to acquire materials unavailable in their home territories.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer because it presents the conclusion that most logically completes the text's discussion of the archaeological evidence from the Peruvian settlement. The text describes a pattern where the coastal site contained both coastal and highland materials, but crucially, the highland materials arrived in processed forms: the alpaca wool "appeared to have arrived already woven" rather than as raw wool that would be processed locally, and 30% of ceramics were "manufactured using clay sources found only in highland regions" rather than made locally from highland clay. This pattern indicates an exchange of finished, specialized products between communities. If the coastal site were simply acquiring raw materials, we would expect to find raw wool being processed there and local ceramics made from imported clay. Instead, the evidence points to communities trading their specialized finished goods—coastal communities producing coastal ceramics and highland communities producing woven textiles and highland ceramics—with the coastal settlement serving as a point where these specialized products were exchanged.

Choice A is incorrect because the text indicates that the wool "appeared to have arrived already woven," meaning coastal communities were receiving finished textiles rather than producing them locally. If coastal communities possessed highland weaving techniques and chose not to use local materials, we would expect to find raw alpaca wool at the site being processed into textiles, but the text explicitly states the wool arrived already woven. Choice C is incorrect because the evidence doesn't support permanent highland settlement in coastal areas. If highland populations had established permanent coastal settlements while maintaining their practices, we would expect to find evidence of wool processing at the coastal site (raw wool, processing tools, evidence of weaving), but the text indicates the wool arrived already woven, suggesting it was processed elsewhere and then transported. Additionally, the presence of both locally-produced coastal ceramics and highland-produced ceramics suggests two distinct communities trading rather than one community maintaining dual traditions. Choice D is incorrect because periodic coastal migration to highland regions would not explain the key pattern described: finished highland products (woven textiles, highland-made ceramics) appearing at the coastal site. Migration for material acquisition would more likely result in raw materials being transported back and processed locally, but the text emphasizes that highland goods arrived in finished form, indicating trade between settled communities rather than migration.

Question Difficulty: Hard

Practice Question 2
Psychologist David Chen observed that students who took brief breaks during long study sessions reported feeling more mentally refreshed and performed better on subsequent tests than students who studied continuously. Suspecting that the timing of these breaks was crucial to their effectiveness, Chen designed an experiment in which participants studied complex material for two hours. He anticipated that students who took breaks at regular 25-minute intervals would thus ______
Which choice most logically completes the text?
A. report feeling more fatigued than students who took breaks at irregular intervals.
B. demonstrate better retention of the material than students who took breaks only when they felt tired.
C. prefer longer study sessions without any breaks in future learning tasks.
D. perform similarly to students who took a single extended break halfway through the session.
✓ Correct Answer: B

Rationale:

Choice B is the best answer. It most logically completes the text. The text establishes that brief breaks during study sessions lead to better outcomes (feeling refreshed and better test performance). Chen suspected that "the timing of these breaks was crucial to their effectiveness," and designed an experiment with breaks at "regular 25-minute intervals." If timing is crucial, we can infer that Chen anticipated that scheduled, regular breaks would be more effective than breaks taken only when students felt tired (which would be irregular and potentially too late to prevent mental fatigue). Thus, students with regularly scheduled breaks would demonstrate better retention than those taking breaks based on feeling tired.

Choice A is incorrect. It doesn't logically complete the text. Chen's hypothesis was that timing matters for break effectiveness. If he anticipated that regular intervals would make students more fatigued than irregular intervals, this would contradict his observation that breaks improve outcomes and his belief that timing is crucial to effectiveness. Choice C is incorrect. It doesn't logically complete the text. The text establishes that breaks improve study outcomes, so Chen would not anticipate that effective breaks would lead students to prefer studying without breaks in the future. Choice D is incorrect. It doesn't logically complete the text. Chen's hypothesis was specifically that "timing of these breaks was crucial," which suggests he believed multiple regular breaks would be more effective than different break patterns. If he anticipated similar performance between regular intervals and a single extended break, this would contradict his premise that timing is crucial.

Question Difficulty: Medium

Key Digital SAT Takeaways: Inferences

  • Read the entire passage carefully before looking at answer choices—you need all the evidence to make the correct inference
  • Look for patterns, contrasts, or relationships between different pieces of information in the passage
  • The correct answer must be supported by evidence in the text, even though it's not directly stated
  • For hypothesis-testing passages, identify what the researcher suspects or expects, then choose the answer that logically follows
  • Eliminate answers that go beyond what the passage supports or contradict information given
  • Pay attention to qualifying words like "rather," "notably," "however"—these often signal important contrasts that lead to the inference
  • Practice distinguishing between what must be true based on the evidence versus what might be true but isn't necessarily supported
